Band History

The Beginning

The founding members of Queen met in West London during the late 1960s. Brian May had built his own guitar with his father in 1963, and formed the group 1984 (named after Orwell's novel) the following year. Roger Taylor, a young dental student, auditioned and got the job as drummer. The band were originally known as Humpy Bong, after former Bee Gees drummer Colin Petersen, but later became known as Queen when they changed their surname to Mercury. They played their first gig in June 1970, playing covers of Cliff Richard's "Please Don't Tease" and other hits.

Early Years

John Deacon joined Queen as bassist n February 1971. In addition to being a seasoned bassist, his quiet demeanour complemented the band, and he was skilled in electronics. On 2 July, Queen played their first show with the classic line-up of Mercury, May, Taylor and Deacon at a Surrey college. They recorded five of their own songs, "Liar", "Keep Yourself Alive", "Great King Rat", "The Night Comes Down" and "Jesus" at De Lane Lea Studios in Wembley. Co-producers Anthony and Baker initially clashed with the band on the direction of the album. "Mad the Swine" was dropped from the running order after the band and Baker could not agree on a mix. Mike Stone created the final mix for "Keep Yourself Alive", and he would go on to work on several other Queen albums. Queen's second album Queen II was released in March 1973. It reached number five on the British album chart and was the first Queen album to chart in the UK. The album featured a 'layered' sound which would become their signature. It is the first of three Queen albums to feature in the book 1001 Albums You Must Hear Before You Die.

Crossing International Waters

Queen's third album, Sheer Heart Attack, was released in 1974. It was their first taste of international success, and went gold in the US. Mercury wrote the closing number, "In the Lap of the Gods", with the intention that the audience could sing along to the chorus when played live. In 2006, Classic Rock ranked it number 28 in "The 100 Greatest British Rock Albums Ever". In January 1975, Queen toured the US as headliners, and played in Canada for the first time. The band then toured Japan from mid-April to the beginning of May. Despite the success, Queen were still tied to the original Trident deal and wages. In August, after an acrimonious split with Trident, the band negotiated themselves out of their contract.

A Night at the Opera was Queen's most expensive album, costing £40,000 and using three different studios. Mercury wrote the opening song "Death on Two Legs", a savage dig at perceived wrongdoers, and the camp vaudeville "Lazing on a Sunday Afternoon" and "Seaside Rendezvous". May's epic "The Prophet's Song" was an eight-minute epic; the middle section is a canon, with simple phrases layered to create a full-choral sound. Mercury's close friend and advisor, Capital London DJ Kenny Everett, played a pivotal role in giving the single exposure. It is the third-best-selling single of all time in the UK, surpassed only by Band Aid's "Do They Know It's Christmas?" and Elton John's "Candle in the Wind".

"Bohemian Rhapsody" was promoted with a music video directed by Bruce Gowers, who had already shot several of Queen's live concerts. Filmed at Elstree Studios in Hertfordshire, the video cost £3,500, five times the typical promotional budget, and was shot in three hours. The operatic section featured a reprise of the Queen II cover, with the band member's heads animated. A Night at the Opera was released in 1975 and went triple platinum in the United States. The second single from the album was Deacon's "You're My Best Friend", which peaked at number 16 on the US Billboard Hot 100, and went on to become a worldwide top-ten hit. It is the third and final Queen album to be featured in the book 1001 Albums You Must Hear Before You Die.

The Startdom Continued

By 1976, Queen were back in the studio recording A Day at the Races. It again borrowed the name of a Marx Brothers movie, and its cover was similar to that of A Night at the Opera. The band self-produced with assistance from Mike Stone, who performed backing vocals. The major hit on the album was "Somebody to Love", a gospel-inspired song in which Mercury, May, and Taylor multi-tracked their voices to create a gospel choir. Queen performed sold-out shows at Madison Square Garden and Earls Court in 1977. Mercury and Taylor used a lighting rig in the shape of a crown for the first time, costing £50,000 to install. The band's sixth studio album News of the World was released in 1977, which has gone four times platinum in the United States, and twice in the UK. In 1978, Queen released Jazz, which reached number two in the UK and number six on the Billboard 200 in the US. "Crazy Little Thing Called Love", a rockabilly inspired song done in the style of Elvis Presley, was the band's first number one single in the United States. Mercury played rhythm guitar while performing the song live, the first time he ever played guitar in concert.

Worldwide Tours

Queen began their 1980s career with The Game. It featured the singles "Crazy Little Thing Called Love" and "Another One Bites the Dust", both of which reached number one in the US. It was also the first appearance of a synthesiser on a Queen album. In 1980, Queen also released the soundtrack they had recorded for Flash Gordon. Queen were the first major rock band to play significant shows in Latin America. One of their concerts in Argentina drew the largest single concert crowd in Argentine history with an audience of 300,000. The group's second show at the Vélez Sarsfield Stadium in Buenos Aires was broadcast on national television and watched by over 30 million. Backstage, the group were introduced to footballer Diego Maradona. Mercury would stop singing and conduct the audience as they took over, with Lesley-Ann Jones writing "the fans knew the song by heart". The ballad "Love of My Life" stole the show in South American concerts. Queen worked with David Bowie on the 1981 single "Under Pressure" Mercury finished the final gig saying, "Adios, amigos, you motherfucksers!". 1982, Queen released the album Hot Space, a departure from their trademark seventies sound, this time being a mixture of pop rock, dance, disco, funk, and R&B. Most of the album was recorded in Munich during the most turbulent period in the band's history. Q magazine would list Hot Space as one of the top fifteen albums where great rock acts lost the plot. The band stopped touring North America after their Hot Space Tour, as their success there had waned. Their fall in popularity in the US has been partially attributed to a homophobia. "They didn't like this identity of Mercury—what they perceived as a brazenly gay rock & roll hero"

That Live Aid Moment

In January 1985, Queen headlined two nights of the first Rock in Rio festival at Rio de Janeiro, Brazil. Highlights from both nights were released on VHS as Queen: Live in Rio. An industry poll in 2005 ranked it the greatest rock performance of all time. At Live Aid, Queen performed some of their greatest hits in front of an estimated 1.9 billion people. In December 2013, Queen's recording studio was opened to the public for the first time. Fans were asked to donate to the Mercury Phoenix Trust charity in exchange for access to the band's archives. In 1985, Queen recorded the album A Kind of Magic, which was successful in the UK and Germany but less so in North America. In 1986, Queen went on the Magic Tour, their final tour with Freddie Mercury. The Magic Tour's highlight was at Wembley Stadium and resulted in the live double album Queen at Wembley. More than one million people saw Queen on the tour—400,000 in the UK alone, a record at the time. At Knebworth Park, over 120,000 fans packed the park for what was Queen's final performance with Mercury.

Mercurys Final Years

Mercury was diagnosed with HIV positive in 1987, but did not make his illness public until 1988. The Miracle spawned the hit singles "I Want It All" and "The Miracle", which became an anti-apartheid anthem in South Africa. In 1990, Queen ended their contract with Capitol and signed with Hollywood Records; they were soon being distributed by Disney. Queen's 1991 album Innuendo is one of the best-selling albums of all time and has sold 16 million copies worldwide. The single "The Show Must Go On", released as a forerunner to Greatest Hits II, featured archive footage of Queen's performances between 1981 and 1989. Mercury was increasingly ill and could barely walk when the band recorded "Innuendo", but singer Brian May says he "completely killed it". "Bohemian Rhapsody" was re-released as a single shortly after Mercury's death, with "These Are the Days of Our Lives" as the double A-side. The single went to number one in the UK, remaining there for five weeks. Initial proceeds from the single—approximately £1,000,000—were donated to the Terrence Higgins Trust, an AIDS charity. In 1992, The Freddie Mercury Tribute Concert was held at London's Wembley Stadium to a crowd of 72,000.

Queen Today

Queen were scheduled to headline Sonisphere at Knebworth on 7 July 2012 with Adam Lambert before the festival was cancelled. Queen also performed with Lambert on 3 July 2012 at Moscow's Olympic Stadium and in Wroclaw, Poland. On 20 September 2013, Queen performed at the iHeartRadio Music Festival at the MGM Grand Hotel & Casino in Las Vegas. One new track, "There Must Be More to Life Than This", is a duet between Mercury and Michael Jackson. Queen + Adam Lambert performed in the shadow of Big Ben in Central Hall, Westminster, central London at the Big Ben New Year concert on New Year's Eve 2014 and 2015. The Queen have released a live album with Adam Lambert titled Live Around the World. In 2016, the group embarked across Europe and Asia on the Queen + Adam Lambert 2016 Summer Festival Tour. This included closing the Isle of Wight Festival in June where they performed "Who Wants to Live Forever" as a tribute to the victims of the Orlando shooting. The band toured North America in the summer of 2017 and toured Europe in late 2017.

On 24 February 2019, Queen + Adam Lambert opened the 91st Academy Awards ceremony held at the Dolby Theatre in Hollywood, Los Angeles. In July 2019 they embarked on the North American leg of The Rhapsody Tour, with the dates sold out in April.They toured Japan and South Korea in January 2020 followed by Australia and New Zealand the following month. On 16 February the band reprised their Live Aid set for the first time in 35 years at the Fire Fight Australia concert at ANZ Stadium in Sydney to raise money for the 2019 Australian bushfire crisis. Because Queen were not able to tour due to the COVID-19 pandemic, they released a live album with Adam Lambert on 2 October 2020. The 20-plus song collection, titled Live Around the World, contains personally selected highlights by the band members from over 200 shows throughout their history. It marked their first live album with Lambert who, as of 2020, has played 218 shows with the band. On 31 December 2020, Queen performed on the Japanese New Year's Eve television special Kōhaku with composer Yoshiki and vocalist Sarah Brightman. In 2021 Queen received the Japan Gold Disc Award for the fourth time (having previously won it in 2005, 2019 and 2020) as the most popular Western act in Japan. On 4 June 2022, Queen + Alan Lambert opened the Platinum Party at the Palace outside Buckingham Palace to mark the Queen's Platinum Jubilee. Performing a three-song set, they opened with "We Will Rock You" which had been introduced in a comedy segment where Queen Elizabeth II and Paddington Bear tapped their tea cups to the beat of the song.
